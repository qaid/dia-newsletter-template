January 29, 2013

2013: Our Dedication to Justice Continues!

Dear CCR Supporter,

As we enter another year as partners in the fight for human rights and true social change, there are grim reminders that more hard work lies ahead, but also signs that our common commitment to justice and fairness has shining moments of achievement. January 2013 has brought us clear examples of both.

 

President Obama, Close Guantanamo!

Four years ago this month, President Barack Obama promised to shut down the notorious prison at Guantánamo Bay within one year. He has broken that promise.

Guantánamo has entered its twelfth year of operation and 166 men are still unjustly detained. Most of them have not and will not ever be charged with any crime. Obama’s second term has begun, and he must act fast to release the men he will not charge and give fair trials to those he will.

On January 11, in Washington, D.C., CCR, along with a broad coalition of human rights groups and other organizations, held a rally, march and vigil to mark the 11th anniversary of the first detentions at Guantanamo. There were also solidarity events in Chicago, Dallas, Detroit, Los Angeles, Miami, and London. To view photos from the DC events, visit CCR’s Facebook page.

Contact the President and tell him that he must fulfill his promise to close Guantanamo. In addition, please visit ccrjustice.org/closegtmo/solidarity to learn about other actions you can take. Your continued efforts to make your voice heard will make closing this shameful chapter in our history possible.

 
 

Update on Sexual Minorities Uganda v. Scott Lively

On January 7, CCR and our client Sexual Minorities Uganda (SMUG) had an important court hearing in our case against president of the conservative evangelical Abiding Truth Ministries. We have sued Lively for his role in the persecution of the LGBTI community in Uganda. Staff from SMUG and CCR, other activists from Uganda, and many activists and supporters from around New England packed the Springfield, MA courtroom and two overflow rooms to watch CCR lawyer Pam Spees argue that the case must be allowed to move forward. We are currently awaiting a decision from the judge.

The day after the hearing, HuffPost Live hosted a segment on the case and Lively’s role in Uganda, featuring CCR’s Pam Spees and SMUG’s Pepe Onziema. You can also see CCR Executive Director Vince Warren’s Guest Voices blog about the hearing Scott Lively Gets His Day in Court in the Washington Post. We are proud to have your support as we continue to pursue justice in this case.

 
 

Justice for Abu Ghraib Victims and Accountability for Private Contractors

In a watershed moment for accountability for corporate human rights violations, defense contractor Engility Holdings, Inc., formerly known as L-3 Services, Inc., paid a settlement to 71 former prisoners at Abu Ghraib and other detention facilities. This is the first time a private military contractor accused of torture has ever compensated the victims. This represents significant progress in the struggle for corporate responsibility and accountability. Read the HuffPost article on the settlement and go to this casepage for more on CCR’s case.

 
 

The long battle for justice for the 9/11 detainees continues

In CCR’s post-9/11 racial and religious profiling case, Turkmen v. Ashcroft, the claims of abuse by the wardens and prison officials at the Metropolitan Detention Center in New York have been allowed to go forward, despite a disappointing ruling this month in which the judge dismissed the claims against the high-level officials who were the architects of the policy. This is a victory to celebrate – the men will finally have their day on court.

 
 

Trespass Stops by NYPD Unconstitutional

In a ruling that confirms what millions already know – that NYPD officers routinely stop New Yorkers without the reasonable suspicion required by the Fourth Amendment -- Judge Shira A. Scheindlin of the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York issued a finding on January 8 that NYPD trespass stops outside of so-called “clean halls” buildings -- for alleged trespasses involving the plaintiffs’ own apartment buildings -- are unconstitutional. Judge Scheindlin’s decision to consolidate that case with CCR’s lawsuit, Floyd et al. v. City of New York, et al., correctly recognizes that these stops are part of a larger problem that has disrupted the lives of New Yorkers, particularly Black and Latino, for more than a decade. CCR looks forward to working jointly to craft meaningful and effective city-wide remedies to address these serious constitutional violations. Visit our website stopandfrisk.org to learn more and hear personal stories on the impact of these stops.

 
 

Finally, in an exciting opportunity, longtime CCR board member and Cuba enthusiast Alex Rosenberg has planned a trip in late February with CCR people in mind. The program will include art, culture, food, music and politics, and a portion of the proceeds will be donated to CCR. A wonderful time will be had by all. For more information on the trip contact info@aflfc.org. The deadline for signing up for the trip is February 1.

None of these developments that lend hope, provide justice, and impel us to fight on as obstacles persist, would be possible without the actions you take, the voices you make heard, the letters you write, and the continued commitment you have to a more just world. We look forward to continued progress this year as we collaborate in that effort.

 

In Solidarity,
Annette Warren Dickerson
Director of Education and Outreach
